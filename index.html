"The first wealth is health."

"Good health and well-being" is a fundamental aspect of human life and is also one of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDG 3). It encompasses physical, mental, and social well-being, aiming for a holistic state of being where individuals can thrive and fulfill their potential. Achieving good health involves access to quality healthcare services, nutritious food, clean water, and adequate sanitation. It also requires promoting mental health, preventing diseases, and ensuring access to essential medicines and vaccines. Well-being goes beyond absence of illness; it includes happiness, fulfillment, and a sense of purpose in life. Factors such as education, employment, social support, and a safe environment contribute significantly to overall well-being.

Facts About Food

Here are some facts about food by the World Health Organization (WHO):
  • Food safety is a global concern affecting people of all ages:Food safety is a pressing global concern that affects individuals of all ages around the world. Unsafe food can have severe health consequences, ranging from mild gastrointestinal discomfort to serious foodborne diseases, which pose a heightened risk to vulnerable populations such as young children, pregnant women, the elderly, and those with weakened immune systems. Beyond health implications, foodborne illnesses also incur substantial economic costs through medical expenses, lost productivity, and the expenses associated with food recalls and investigations. Addressing food safety requires international collaboration to ensure that food is safe throughout its production, processing, storage, and preparation stages. Preventive measures advocated by organizations like the World Health Organization (WHO) include promoting proper hygiene practices, ensuring thorough cooking of food, maintaining safe water supplies, regulating storage temperatures, and implementing effective food handling and preparation techniques. Countries often establish regulatory frameworks aligned with international standards set by WHO and the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) to safeguard food safety. By raising awareness and implementing robust measures, global efforts can mitigate the risks associated with unsafe food practices and protect public health worldwide.
  • Poor food safety practices can lead to diseases such as diarrhea:Poor food safety practices significantly increase the risk of contracting diseases such as diarrhea, highlighting the critical importance of proper food handling and hygiene. Diarrhea is a common consequence of consuming contaminated food or water contaminated with pathogens such as bacteria, viruses, or parasites. Symptoms can range from mild discomfort to severe dehydration and even death, particularly in vulnerable populations like young children and individuals with compromised immune systems. Preventing foodborne illnesses like diarrhea requires adherence to strict food safety measures throughout the food chain, from production and processing to preparation and consumption. Effective strategies include ensuring cleanliness during food handling, cooking food thoroughly to kill harmful microorganisms, maintaining proper storage temperatures, and using safe water sources. Public health agencies, including the World Health Organization (WHO), emphasize education and enforcement of food safety regulations to reduce the incidence of foodborne diseases and safeguard public health globally.
